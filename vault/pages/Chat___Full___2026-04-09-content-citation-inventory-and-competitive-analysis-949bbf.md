## Report delivered. Here's what you need to know:

**The uncomfortable truth:** You're beating AO in total citations (+32%), but you're losing the war that matters. Here's why:

### Your actual problem isn't volume—it's **dilution and positioning**

**TGG:** 10,121 citations spread across 2,703 URLs = 3.7 citations/URL  
**AO:** 7,650 citations across 1,329 URLs = 5.8 citations/URL

AO is getting **55% more citation efficiency** per URL. They're winning with fewer, better-targeted assets.

### The content type gap is killing you

You have 719 buying guides but **zero formal blog posts**. AO has 419 blog posts (32% of their content). 

**Why this matters:** AI platforms are citing blogs for informational queries ("how to choose", "what's the difference"), which drive top-of-funnel awareness. You're invisible in that space.

### Your "win" is actually exposure to risk

Your top citations are all price-driven: "Where can I buy **affordable** [product]?" 

- Cheap dryers: 100 cites
- Cheap headphones: 49 cites  
- Clearance pages: high performance

**The problem:** You're being positioned as the **discount retailer**, not the authority. That's fine until price isn't the differentiator anymore—then you have no moat.

### The 5 high-priority gaps are symptoms, not causes

Soundbars, headphones, speakers—yes, create those comparison reviews. But that's table stakes. The bigger issue:

**You don't have a content model that competes with RTINGS, CHOICE, or Consumer Reports.**

Those publishers dominate because they have:
- Testing methodologies (you don't)
- Regular update cadence (you don't)  
- Structured comparison data (you don't)

Creating 5 "Best X Australia" articles won't move the needle if they're just repackaged product lists.

### What you should actually do

**Stop avoiding the hard question:** Are you willing to invest in a testing/review infrastructure, or are you going to keep writing buying guides that cite manufacturer specs?

Because right now:
- 45 of your URLs have high citations but "unclear content type" (the "Other" bucket)
- That means they're getting cited by accident, not by design
- You're relying on category pages and clearance URLs to carry you

**The Excel shows:**
- Which URLs can be pivoted (45 opportunities)  
- What specific content is missing by topic
- Citation density per URL (your efficiency problem)
- Exact prompts driving citations (mostly price-focused)

**The text file shows:**  
- Phased action plan (quick wins → strategic content → blog infrastructure)
- URL strategy (which existing content to expand vs create new)
- Competitor content patterns you need to mirror

### My recommendation

**Phase 1 (now):** Audit those 45 high-citation "Other" URLs and convert them into structured buying guides with comparison tables and FAQ schema. This costs you **nothing** but editor time and could 2x their citation efficiency.

**Phase 2 (next):** Before you create new "Best X" content, decide if you're going to actually test products or just aggregate. If you're aggregating, you're competing with Reddit and ProductReview.com.au—not RTINGS. Calibrate expectations accordingly.

**Phase 3 (later):** Launch a blog. Not "articles" in /whats-new. An actual /blog with seasonal content, how-to guides, and X vs Y comparisons. AO is eating your lunch here.
