Honestly, no. Not significantly. Here's why, referencing the doc:

**Answer-First Format** — the goal is to always answer the main intent or question in the first 200 words. These lines do that for a human reader, but they're preview/header copy sitting above an image. The image carries the mechanic detail, so the text alone can't be extracted by an AI and understood in isolation.

**Semantic Triplets** — clear declarative language that facilitates the AI's understanding of relationships works best as Subject → Predicate → Object. "Get $40 or $60 off instantly when you spend $300 or $400+" is close, but compressed. An AI parsing it has to infer two separate offer tiers from a single clause.

**Semantic Passages** — short self-contained sections focusing on one idea work best when, if an AI cited the header and paragraph together, all the information needed to understand it would be contained within that paragraph. One line of preview copy above an image fails this test entirely. The full mechanic lives in the artwork, which an AI cannot read.

**The honest answer:** AI optimisation at this level applies to blog intros, PLP copy, and body content, not to a single teaser line above a promotional banner. The doc's principles are designed for extractable, standalone text blocks. This copy isn't that format, so the framework doesn't really apply here.
