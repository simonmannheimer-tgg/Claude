# Australian Retail Language

Phrases and context notes for TGG long-form content. These augment the base content from `tgg-content-strategist` — they are not a replacement for natural writing. Use where they fit naturally; do not force them.

## Must-use phrases (where applicable to the topic)

| Phrase | When to use |
|---|---|
| click and collect | When discussing purchase or fulfilment options |
| interest-free | When mentioning finance (do not specify rates — they change) |
| finance options | Generic reference to TGG payment flexibility |
| energy star rating | When discussing appliance efficiency (use the correct label for the product) |
| energy efficiency | General efficiency discussion |
| running costs | Ongoing cost of ownership — preferred over "operating costs" |
| Australian standard | When citing safety or performance standards |
| warranty | Use "warranty" not "guarantee" for product coverage |
| trade-in | When discussing upgrades and disposal of old appliances |

## Australian retail context notes

- Prices are GST-inclusive. Never mention GST-exclusive pricing.
- Finance: reference "interest-free options" or "finance options" — do not quote specific interest rates or minimum spends (these change with promotions).
- Energy ratings: Australia uses the Energy Star Rating system (1–10 stars for most appliances). Reference star counts only if you can verify the current rating for a specific model.
- Standards: reference "Australian Standards" (AS prefix) where relevant. Common ones: AS/NZS 3000 (electrical wiring), AS/NZS 60335 (household appliances).
- Disposal: reference "council recycling" or "appliance disposal" — not "junk removal" (US term).
- Voltage: Australia uses 230V/50Hz. Relevant when discussing imported or dual-voltage products.

## Phrases to avoid in TGG content

- "Check out" (too casual)
- "Amazing" / "incredible" (unsubstantiated superlatives)
- "Best-in-class" (needs evidence)
- "Innovative" (overused, rarely meaningful)
- "Seamless" (AI cliché)
- "Cutting-edge" (overused)
- "State-of-the-art" (overused)
- "Game-changer" (overused)
- "Leverage" (corporate jargon)
- "Going forward" (corporate jargon)
